Key Concepts Explained

1 The Regression Equation: $y = a + bx$

  • • $x$ (Independent/Explanatory): The variable you control, select, or change. Plotted on the bottom horizontal axis.
  • • $y$ (Dependent/Response): The variable you measure as a response. Plotted on the vertical side axis.
  • • Intercept ($a$): The starting value of $y$ when $x = 0$.
  • • Gradient ($b$): The rate of change. It tells you exactly how much $y$ increases (or decreases, if negative) for every single unit increase of $x$.

2 Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

Interpolation means predicting a value inside your collected data range. Because we have data representing this interval, it is generally considered reliable.

Extrapolation means predicting outside the collected data range. This is highly unreliable because you cannot prove the linear trend will continue beyond the original boundaries.

3 Direction of Prediction Rule

A regression line of $y$ on $x$ is specially calculated to predict $y$ when you are given $x$. It does this by making the vertical distances (errors in $y$) between the real points and the line as small as possible.

Why can't we rearrange the formula to find $x$?

If you wanted to predict $x$ from $y$, you would need to minimize horizontal errors instead. That requires a completely different line (called the $x$ on $y$ line).

Do not rearrange the $y = a + bx$ equation algebraically to find $x$. Doing so will result in mathematically incorrect and highly inaccurate predictions!

Example 1 Variant

Interpreting Real-world Intercepts & Gradients

A business researcher models the relationship between the age of a commercial delivery van, x years, and its resale value, y pounds (£).

The least squares regression line equation is calculated as:

y = 28500 - 2400x
£
£

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Understanding the Model: The equation is in the form $y = a + bx$, where $y$ is the value in pounds, and $x$ is the age in years.

(a) The intercept ($a$): When the vehicle is brand new, its age $x = 0$. Substituting $x = 0$ into the equation yields:
y = 28500 - 2400(0) = £28,500 Therefore, the brand new starting value is exactly the intercept term: £28500.

(b) The gradient ($b$): The coefficient of $x$ is $-2400$. The negative sign represents a decrease. For every unit step increase in $x$ (every additional year), $y$ decreases by exactly £2400.

Example 2 Variant

Predictions & Large Data Set Boundaries

A student uses records from the Large Data Set to investigate daily mean temperature, $t\text{ °C}$, and daily total sunshine, $s\text{ hours}$, at an overseas location.

The original data only contains days where the temperature ranges between 5°C and 22°C.

The calculated regression line equation of $s$ on $t$ is:

s = -2.1 + 0.58t
hours

Step-by-Step Breakdown

(a) Substituting $t = $ 12:
s = -2.1 + 0.58(12) = 4.86 hours

(b) Interpolation vs Extrapolation: Since 12°C lies fully within the sample data range (5°C to 22°C), this prediction is safe and reliable.

(c) Why this temperature fails: Substituting $t = $ 2 yields:
s = -2.1 + 0.58(2) = -0.94 hours A negative duration of sunshine is a physical impossibility, illustrating the dangers of extrapolation.

(d) Rearrangement Limit: The least-squares procedure is asymmetric. A regression model designed for the response $s$ given covariate $t$ minimizes squared vertical differences. Swapping the roles algebraically fails to yield the true line of $t$ on $s$.

Example 3 Variant

Decoding Coded Variables (Plain English)

To make extremely large data numbers easier to handle, a statistical package codes raw data variables, $x$ and $y$, into smaller variables, $p$ and $q$, using these formulas:

p = x - 4
q = y / 20

The regression line for the simplified, coded variables is found to be:

q = 5.4 + 2.1p

Find the final decoded regression equation of $y$ on $x$. Write your decoded equation in the form: $y = a + bx$.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Step 1: Substitute formulas in place of variables

Replace $q$ with $y \div$ 20 and replace $p$ with $(x -$ 4$)$:

y / 20 = 5.4 + 2.1(x - 4)
Step 2: Isolate the variable $y$

Multiply all terms on the right side by 20 to remove the fractional denominator:

y = 20 × [ 5.4 + 2.1(x - 4) ]

Evaluating products: 20 × 5.4 = 108 and 20 × 2.1 = 42.

y = 108 + 42(x - 4)
Step 3: Expand brackets and simplify

Multiply the gradient term through the parenthetical binomial:

y = 108 + 42x - 168

Combine constant integers (108 − 168 = −60):

y = -60 + 42x
Example 4 Variant

Modeling Limits & Intercept Boundaries

A community gym is tracking membership metrics. The manager registers the monthly subscription price, $x\text{ pounds}$, and the total active member count, $y$.

Data is gathered strictly from test price structures ranging between £15 and £45.

The calculated regression line is:

y = 650 - 8x
members

Step-by-Step Breakdown

(a) Membership Prediction: Plug $x = $ 25 directly into the linear equation:
y = 650 - 8(25) = 450 members

(b) Gradient Interpretation: The gradient is $-$8. In plain terms, for each additional £1 added to the subscription cost, the customer base drops by 8 active people.

(c) Boundary Validity: A price of $x = 0$ (free access) is far below the lower boundary of the experimental data (£15). This represents extrapolation, which cannot be assumed to remain linear.
